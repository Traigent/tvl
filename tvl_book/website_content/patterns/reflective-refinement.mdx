Reflective refinement is the depth-scaling pattern most teams reach for early. The tempting version
is informal: ask the model to think again. The operational version is stricter. Reflection is a
budgeted loop with explicit tuned variables governing how critique happens, what is remembered, and
when the loop stops.

## The Practical Question

The real question is not whether reflection sounds smart. It is whether one or two additional passes
produce enough measurable lift to justify the extra cost and latency.

That means tuning:

- how many revision rounds are allowed
- whether critiques come from a rubric, failure trace, or alternate perspective
- which state is forwarded into the next round
- which condition terminates the loop

<ConceptCallout title="Useful Reframe" tone="info">
  Reflection is not a personality trait of the agent. It is a governed controller with a finite
  budget and an explicit stop rule.
</ConceptCallout>

## Why It Works

First-pass drafts often fail for systematic reasons:

- they overlook a constraint
- they optimize the wrong subgoal
- they overfit to one interpretation of the prompt

Reflection can help because it creates a structured chance to surface those mistakes before the
system commits to output.

```yaml
tvars:
  - name: reflection_rounds
    type: int
    domain:
      set: [0, 1, 2]
  - name: critique_mode
    type: enum[str]
    domain: ["rubric", "failure_trace", "contrastive_view"]
  - name: stop_rule
    type: enum[str]
    domain: ["fixed_budget", "no_change", "score_plateau"]
```

## Where Teams Go Wrong

Reflection becomes wasteful when it is under-specified. Without a clear critique mode and stop
criterion, the system often turns one useful pass into several near-duplicate passes.

<PitfallCard title="Loop Without Signal">
  If the critique cannot point to a concrete defect or measurable rubric miss, extra rounds usually
  add style variation rather than substantive correction.
</PitfallCard>

## Operational Checklist

- Compare zero-round, one-round, and two-round settings explicitly.
- Measure marginal lift per round rather than only best observed performance.
- Keep critique prompts inspectable so failures can be diagnosed.
- Treat “no further gain” as a valid stopping outcome, not as a failure to reflect.

<KnowledgeCheck prompt="What makes reflective refinement governable instead of open-ended?">
  It has an explicit loop budget, a defined critique mechanism, and a stop rule that can be tested
  against cost, latency, and quality outcomes.
</KnowledgeCheck>
