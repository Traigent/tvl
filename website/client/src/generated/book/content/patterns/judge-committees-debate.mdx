Judge committees and debate patterns introduce disagreement on purpose. Instead of trusting a single
answer or a single evaluation pass, the system creates multiple views, then tunes how those views are
compared, argued over, and collapsed into a decision.

## Two Common Variants

1. Solver-side debate: several solvers produce or defend answers over one or more rounds.
2. Judge-side committees: several evaluators score or rank candidate outputs, sometimes with dynamic
team selection.

The shared engineering idea is controlled disagreement plus governed aggregation.

<ConceptCallout title="Why Teams Use It" tone="tip">
  When correctness is hard to define procedurally, committees become a practical alternative to both
  single-sample outputs and constant human review.
</ConceptCallout>

## What Needs Tuning

The pattern becomes expensive quickly, so its control surface matters:

- how many agents or judges participate
- how many rounds are allowed
- whether agents see each other’s arguments directly
- which rubric governs scoring
- how ties, splits, or abstentions are handled

## What Makes It Operationally Tricky

Disagreement is only useful if the system can tell whether it reflects:

- real uncertainty about the task
- genuine perspective diversity
- or just noisy restatements of the same mistake

That is why committee composition and aggregation rules should be visible in the evidence bundle, not
buried in prompt text.

<PitfallCard title="Cosmetic Committees">
  A committee of nearly identical judges can create the appearance of rigor without improving
  calibration. Diversity source and aggregation policy matter at least as much as committee size.
</PitfallCard>

## Operational Checklist

- Measure agreement rate and disagreement resolution rate, not just final vote.
- Log minority positions when promotion decisions are close.
- Compare static committees with dynamic judge selection explicitly.
- Decide ahead of time whether split decisions should promote, retry, or escalate.

<KnowledgeCheck prompt="Why is aggregation policy central in committee and debate patterns?">
  Because disagreement alone does not improve a system. The operational value comes from how that
  disagreement is interpreted, scored, and turned into a governed release or answer decision.
</KnowledgeCheck>
