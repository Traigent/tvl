The simplest way to reason about a pattern bundle is to split it into two layers:

- scaffold: the stable workflow structure that already works on its own
- controller: the extra mechanism that adds search, reflection, judging, or another adaptive policy

This matches the way many effective systems are built. A narrow-waist base does the core work, and
one bounded controller is allowed to change the operating point.

<ConceptCallout title="Production Heuristic" tone="integration">

Prefer one clear scaffold plus one clear controller before trying several interacting controllers at
 once.

</ConceptCallout>

This is also how you keep the study plane interpretable. If the scaffold and controller are both
changing at the same time, it becomes harder to explain where the gains came from or why the bundle
failed.

```yaml
tvars:
  - name: scaffold_variant
    type: enum[str]
    domain: ["scaffold_only", "scaffold_plus_reflect", "scaffold_plus_judge"]
  - name: controller_budget
    type: int
    domain:
      set: [1, 2, 4]
```

<KnowledgeCheck prompt="Why is a scaffold-versus-controller split useful in multi-pattern systems?">

It keeps the bundle interpretable. Reviewers can ask whether the stable workflow is strong on its
own and whether the controller adds enough lift to justify its extra cost and complexity.

</KnowledgeCheck>
