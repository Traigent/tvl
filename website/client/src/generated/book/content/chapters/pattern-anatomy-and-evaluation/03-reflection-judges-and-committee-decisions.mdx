[Reflective Refinement](/book/patterns/reflective-refinement) and
[Judge Committees / Debate](/book/patterns/judge-committees-debate) are often grouped together
because both add more reasoning work after a first pass. But their jobs are different.

- reflection asks: can one draft improve through bounded self-critique?
- committee or debate asks: should several views argue or vote before a decision is accepted?

Reflection is primarily a revision controller. Committee patterns are primarily uncertainty-management
and arbitration mechanisms.

<ConceptCallout title="Decision Rule" tone="info">

If the main risk is a weak first draft, evaluate reflection first. If the main risk is ambiguous or
disputed correctness, committees and judges deserve earlier budget.

</ConceptCallout>

Their evidence expectations differ too:

- reflection should show marginal lift per added round
- committees should show better calibration, not just more activity

Without that distinction, teams can spend several extra calls and still learn very little about why a
workflow improved.

<PitfallCard title="More Rounds, Same Mistake">

If a reflection loop keeps preserving the assumptions of the first draft, more rounds only increase
latency. Likewise, a committee of highly correlated judges can create the appearance of rigor without
adding real calibration.

</PitfallCard>

<KnowledgeCheck prompt="What is the key difference between reflection and committee patterns?">

Reflection is a bounded revise loop over one evolving answer. Committee and debate patterns use
controlled disagreement across several views or judges before accepting a decision.

</KnowledgeCheck>
