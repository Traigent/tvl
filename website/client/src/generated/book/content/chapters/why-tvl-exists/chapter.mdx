TVL grew out of a simple observation: **AI systems do not live in a static world**. Models change,
latency shifts with traffic, provider prices move, and product expectations tighten after deployment.
Hard-coded configuration pushes teams toward one of two bad habits: leave too much risk hidden in code,
or keep making ad hoc changes with no stable contract to review.

TVL reframes that situation. Instead of treating agent behavior as a pile of scattered config and prompt
edits, it gives product and engineering one governed artifact that says what may vary, what must stay safe,
and what evidence is required before change ships.

<ConceptCallout title="TVL in One Sentence" tone="info">

TVL is a machine-readable contract for “what can change, what must stay safe, and what evidence is required
before a better configuration ships.”

</ConceptCallout>

<ConceptCallout title="Why Teams Adopt It" tone="hint">

- Product teams can declare goals, guardrails, and rollout expectations explicitly.
- Engineering teams can tune, validate, and ship against that same contract.
- Reviewers can tell the difference between design choices, environment assumptions, and promotion evidence.

</ConceptCallout>
